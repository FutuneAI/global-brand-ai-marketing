## Core Argument

The standard global marketing stack — website, content, distribution channels — has structural gaps that make brands invisible to AI retrieval systems. These gaps are not about budget or effort; they are about information architecture. The standard stack was designed for a world where buyers begin with search engines. A new layer has emerged in front of that world, and most brands have nothing built there.

The three existing layers each have a distinct failure mode:
- **Website layer:** Low signal-to-noise ratio; content is semantically generic and fails retrieval candidacy
- **Content layer:** Volume without semantic density; machine-translated or generic content occupies the wrong position in embedding space
- **Distribution layer:** Platform reach ≠ training data coverage ≠ retrieval candidacy — these are three different things, commonly conflated

Above these three layers: GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) as the fourth layer, and AI-native distribution as an emerging fifth layer operating upstream of retrieval itself.

## Article Structure Summary

1. **The behavior change** — AI as a pre-search category filter in B2B decisions; not confined to complex purchases
2. **How AI actually decides whether to mention you** — three mechanisms (training / RAG / sampling); the false causality of "fix structured data → get mentioned"
3. **Three infrastructure layers, three failure modes**
   - Website: semantic genericism; Schema.org as amplifier not gate
   - Content: embedding position problem; why translation quality is a symptom not the cause
   - Distribution: reach ≠ training coverage ≠ retrieval; LinkedIn vs. industry media vs. corporate blog
4. **The fifth layer: AI-native distribution** — distinct from GEO; entering source pools rather than optimizing for retrieval from them
5. **Prioritization** — infrastructure layers set the ceiling; single-point strengths can break through; sequence matters
